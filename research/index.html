Research

Methodology Link to heading

My methodological work aims to do two things: (1) resolve methodological problems encountered in my substantive work and (2) (more interestingly) understand the relation between sociological theorizing and quantitative research. In particular, I’m investigating how semantic theories can be substantiated with statistical modeling and how quantitative statistical research can be applied to abductive theorizing.

  • (under review) Schultz, Michael. “A Nonparameteric Framework for Modeling Fields and Social Spaces.” (replication package coming soon)

Organizations and Structures of Communication: Link to heading

My work on the effects of communications structures uses text analysis, information theory, and social networks to investigate the relationships between interactions and social structures in conversations, discussions, and other forms of organizational communications.

Relevant Work:

  • Schultz, Michael, Leslie DeChurch, and Noshir Contractor. “Embedded Interactions and Selective Disclosure: Network Effects on Conversations aboard Skylab.” Conditionally accepted at Symbolic Interaction.

  • (working paper) Schultz, Michael and Jonah Stuart Brundage. “The Transformation of Global Diplomacy: Networks of Diplomatic Missions, 1648-1815.”

  • (under review) Schultz, Michael. “Trajectories of Deliberation: The Effects of Ambiguity, Dissent, and Transparency on Discussions at the FOMC.”

  • Fligstein, Neil, Jonah Stuart Brundage, and Michael Schultz. 2017. “Seeing Like the Fed: Culture, Cognition, and Framing in the Failure to Anticipate the Financial Crisis of 2008.” American Sociological Review 82(5): 879-909.

  • Feinberg, Matthew, Robb Willer, and Michael Schultz. 2014. “Gossip and Ostracism Promote Cooperation in Groups.” Psychological Science 25(3): 656-664.
